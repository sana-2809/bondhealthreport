\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{ch:literature}

The integration of artificial intelligence into document processing and diagram recognition has advanced significantly through progress in deep learning, multimodal AI, and intelligent document understanding. These developments have fostered a broader ecosystem of AI applications in academic and professional workflows, facilitating more efficient information synthesis. Research has shifted from rule-based document analysis toward data-driven approaches capable of handling complex visual information, employing CNNs for shape detection, OCR for text extraction, transformer-based models for contextual understanding, and graph-based methods for structural modelling.~\cite{ai-education}

%---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Computer Vision for Diagram Recognition}
\label{sec:cv-recognition}
%---------------------------------------------------------------

Vishnu Vardhan Reddy and Rajeswari propose a unified framework that 
integrates optical character recognition with generative AI for 
document understanding. Their approach emphasizes preprocessing 
techniques such as noise reduction, binarization, and layout 
analysis to enhance recognition accuracy. However, the approach targets text-centric documents and lacks mechanisms for detecting geometric shapes, directional arrows, or inter-node relationships. Furthermore, the output remains static and does not support post-recognition editing or user interaction.~\cite{ocr-review}

A more recent survey on graphical object detection highlights that 
standard OCR pipelines are insufficient for interpreting graphical 
elements such as diagrams, tables, and figures, necessitating 
specialized detection models based on deep neural networks. These 
approaches treat diagram components as visual objects requiring 
dedicated detection and classification pipelines rather than simple 
text extraction tasks. The performance of such systems is often predicated on the use of robust machine learning frameworks like TensorFlow, enabling the deployment of efficient backbone architectures such as MobileNetV2 for real-time inference. Furthermore, conventional vision libraries like OpenCV provide the foundational image processing routines required for these pipelines.~\cite{opencv,mobilenet,tensorflow}

Liu and Zhang further categorize various diagram recognition methods into symbol-based and relation-based approaches. They emphasize that while symbol recognition has 
reached high maturity using deep learning models, the automated 
detection of complex structural relationships remains an active 
area of research. Their survey highlights the potential of combining 
domain-specific heuristics with neural networks to bridge the gap 
between pixel-level detection and semantic graph 
reconstruction.~\cite{diagram-recognition}

%---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Handwriting Recognition in Document Processing}
\label{sec:handwriting}
%---------------------------------------------------------------

Deep learning-based handwriting recognition has been extensively 
studied, with CNNs, recurrent neural networks (RNNs), and 
connectionist temporal classification (CTC) forming the core 
architectures. CNNs are particularly effective in learning spatial 
features directly from image data, enabling high accuracy in both 
printed and handwritten text recognition tasks.Transformer-based models have further improved contextual understanding.~\cite{szeliski}

However, handwriting recognition systems exhibit notable limitations 
when applied to diagram labels. Variability in handwriting styles, 
image quality, and lighting conditions reduces robustness. Moreover, 
these systems treat text as a linear sequence, failing to preserve 
spatial formatting such as multi-line labels within diagram nodes. 
This loss of formatting is critical, as spatial arrangement often 
conveys semantic meaning in diagrams.

\section{AI-Powered Code and Flowchart Generation}
\label{sec:ai-generation}
%---------------------------------------------------------------

Large language models (LLMs), including GPT and Google's Gemini, 
have enabled automated flowchart generation from natural language 
descriptions by decomposing textual input into logical 
steps and mapping them to structured representations such as JSON or 
XML. Recent research demonstrates that combining generative AI with 
vision-based preprocessing significantly enhances diagram parsing. 
For instance, the GenFlowchart framework integrates OCR, shape 
detection, and transformer-based language models to produce 
high-quality structured outputs. Despite these advancements, LLM-based systems face challenges such as inconsistent outputs, difficulty handling complex nested logic, and 
occasional generation of invalid structured data. These issues 
necessitate robust validation and error-handling mechanisms.~\cite{flowchart-code}

\section{Interactive Diagramming Tools and Libraries}
\label{sec:diagramming-tools}
%---------------------------------------------------------------
Interactive libraries such as React Flow, GoJS, and mxGraph support 
drag-and-drop, zooming, and export, but primarily focus on manual 
diagram creation and do not integrate automated recognition capabilities. 
Performance limitations arise when rendering large-scale diagrams, and 
advanced features such as undo/redo and custom constraints require 
additional implementation effort. These platforms typically leverage RESTful architectural patterns to manage states and services in a decoupled, scalable manner.~\cite{rest}

The proposed system employs a column-based auto-layout approach, 
where nodes are organized into structured vertical columns based on 
their logical relationships. This method improves readability by 
maintaining alignment and reducing visual clutter, while ensuring 
a consistent flow of information across the diagram. Compared to 
traditional layered layouts, column-based layouts can produce more 
compact and visually balanced representations with fewer edge 
crossings.~\cite{diagram-survey}

\section{Optical Graph Recognition Techniques}
\label{sec:ogr}
%---------------------------------------------------------------

Optical Graph Recognition (OGR) focuses on converting diagram images 
into structured graph representations. Traditional approaches rely 
on computer vision techniques such as contour detection, and heuristic rules for shape and edge detection. Recent deep learning approaches such as Arrow R-CNN extends Faster R-CNN with keypoint detection for arrows, enabling joint symbol and connection recognition.~\cite{arrow-rcnn}

Similarly, Kim and Lee propose a system for automatic flowchart 
recognition from hand-drawn images, utilizing contour analysis and 
shape matching algorithms to identify decision boxes, processes, 
and terminators. Their research highlights the importance of 
handling noise and irregular strokes in hand-drawn diagrams to 
ensure robust structural extraction.~\cite{flowchart-analysis}

Transformer-based models such as FR-DETR achieve high 
precision and recall by combining object detection with line 
segment detection in a unified architecture, reaching F1-scores 
above 90\% on benchmark datasets. Despite these advances, most OGR systems produce static outputs and struggle with real-world hand-drawn conditions.~\cite{diagram-survey}
