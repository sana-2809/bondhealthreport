\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{ch:literature}

The integration of artificial intelligence into document processing 
and diagram recognition has experienced significant advancement, 
driven by progress in deep learning, multimodal AI, and intelligent 
document understanding systems. Modern research has increasingly 
focused on enabling machines to interpret not only textual content 
but also graphical structures and semantic context embedded within diagrams. This evolution reflects a shift from traditional rule-based document analysis toward data-driven 
approaches capable of handling complex visual information.

Recent studies explore a wide range of techniques for diagram 
digitization, including convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for 
object and shape detection, optical character recognition (OCR) 
systems for extracting textual content, transformer-based models 
for contextual understanding, and graph-based methods for modeling 
structural relationships. Additionally, interactive web-based 
diagramming frameworks have been developed to support visualization 
and manipulation of structured outputs.

Despite these advancements, the literature consistently highlights 
several unresolved challenges. These include reduced accuracy in 
hand-drawn or noisy diagrams, difficulty in detecting connections 
and directional edges, limitations in handling overlapping or 
irregular shapes, and the absence of unified systems that combine 
automated recognition with interactive editing capabilities. These 
limitations provide the primary motivation for the development of 
the Automated Diagram Digitization system.

\section{Computer Vision for Diagram Recognition}
\label{sec:cv-recognition}
%---------------------------------------------------------------

Vishnu Vardhan Reddy and Rajeswari propose a unified framework that 
integrates optical character recognition with generative AI for 
document understanding. Their approach emphasizes preprocessing 
techniques such as noise reduction, binarization, and layout 
analysis to enhance recognition accuracy. By incorporating 
language models, the system produces semantically structured outputs 
from raw document images, demonstrating improved extraction accuracy 
for structured documents when preprocessing is applied.

However, this framework primarily targets text-centric documents 
and does not adequately address diagrammatic structures. It lacks 
mechanisms for detecting geometric shapes, directional arrows, 
and inter-node relationships, which are essential for flowchart 
digitization. Furthermore, the output remains static and does not 
support post-recognition editing or user interaction.

A more recent survey on graphical object detection highlights that 
standard OCR pipelines are insufficient for interpreting graphical 
elements such as diagrams, tables, and figures, necessitating 
specialized detection models based on deep neural networks. These 
approaches treat diagram components as visual objects requiring 
dedicated detection and classification pipelines rather than simple 
text extraction tasks.~\cite{ocr-review}

This work supports the use of preprocessing pipelines and 
multimodal AI in the proposed system. The preprocessing techniques 
directly inform the image normalization steps used before invoking 
the Gemini API, while the emphasis on structured output aligns with 
the graph-based representation adopted in this project.

In a more comprehensive study, Liu and Zhang categorize various 
diagram recognition methods into symbol-based and relation-based 
approaches. They emphasize that while symbol recognition has 
reached high maturity using deep learning models, the automated 
detection of complex structural relationships remains an active 
area of research. Their survey highlights the potential of combining 
domain-specific heuristics with neural networks to bridge the gap 
between pixel-level detection and semantic graph 
reconstruction.~\cite{diagram-recognition}

\section{Handwriting Recognition in Document Processing}
\label{sec:handwriting}
%---------------------------------------------------------------

Deep learning-based handwriting recognition has been extensively 
studied, with CNNs, recurrent neural networks (RNNs), and 
connectionist temporal classification (CTC) forming the core 
architectures. CNNs are particularly effective in learning spatial 
features directly from image data, enabling high accuracy in both 
printed and handwritten text recognition tasks.~\cite{szeliski}

More recent advancements incorporate transformer-based architectures, 
which improve recognition by capturing long-range dependencies and 
contextual relationships between characters. These models have 
significantly outperformed traditional sequence-based approaches 
in benchmark datasets.

However, handwriting recognition systems exhibit notable limitations 
when applied to diagram labels. Variability in handwriting styles, 
image quality, and lighting conditions reduces robustness. Moreover, 
these systems treat text as a linear sequence, failing to preserve 
spatial formatting such as multi-line labels within diagram nodes. 
This loss of formatting is critical, as spatial arrangement often 
conveys semantic meaning in diagrams.

The proposed system addresses this limitation through prompt 
engineering strategies that explicitly preserve formatting using 
\texttt{\textbackslash n} characters, ensuring accurate reconstruction 
of multi-line labels.

\section{AI-Powered Code and Flowchart Generation}
\label{sec:ai-generation}
%---------------------------------------------------------------

Large language models (LLMs), including GPT and Google's Gemini, 
have enabled automated flowchart generation from natural language 
descriptions. These models decompose textual input into logical 
steps and map them to structured representations such as JSON or 
XML, effectively bridging conceptual design and visual representation.

Recent research demonstrates that combining generative AI with 
vision-based preprocessing significantly enhances diagram parsing. 
For instance, the GenFlowchart framework integrates OCR, shape 
detection, and transformer-based language models to produce 
high-quality structured outputs, achieving strong semantic matching 
scores and user satisfaction metrics.

Despite these advancements, LLM-based systems face challenges such as 
inconsistent outputs, difficulty handling complex nested logic, and 
occasional generation of invalid structured data. These issues 
necessitate robust validation and error-handling mechanisms.~\cite{flowchart-code}

The proposed system directly addresses these challenges through 
regex-based JSON extraction fallback and automatic edge ID 
generation, ensuring structural consistency.

\section{Interactive Diagramming Tools and Libraries}
\label{sec:diagramming-tools}
%---------------------------------------------------------------

Interactive diagramming tools such as React Flow, GoJS, and mxGraph 
provide powerful interfaces for creating and editing diagrams. These 
libraries support essential features such as drag-and-drop node 
placement, edge connections, zooming, and export functionality.

However, existing tools primarily focus on manual diagram creation 
and do not integrate automated recognition capabilities. Performance 
limitations arise when rendering large-scale diagrams, and advanced 
features such as undo/redo and custom constraints require additional 
implementation effort.

The integration of the Dagre layout engine, based on the Sugiyama 
framework, addresses automatic node positioning by organizing graphs 
into hierarchical layers and minimizing edge crossings. This ensures 
clear and readable diagram layouts.~\cite{diagram-survey}

\section{Optical Graph Recognition Techniques}
\label{sec:ogr}
%---------------------------------------------------------------

Optical Graph Recognition (OGR) focuses on converting diagram images 
into structured graph representations. Traditional approaches rely 
on computer vision techniques such as Hough transforms, contour 
detection, and heuristic rules for shape and edge detection.

Recent deep learning approaches have significantly improved 
performance. For example, Arrow R-CNN extends Faster R-CNN by 
introducing keypoint detection for arrows, enabling joint recognition 
of symbols and connections. This approach improves diagram 
recognition accuracy by reconstructing both structure and semantics 
simultaneously, particularly in handwritten contexts where arrow heads 
and shafts exhibit high variability.~\cite{arrow-rcnn}

Similarly, Kim and Lee propose a system for automatic flowchart 
recognition from hand-drawn images, utilizing contour analysis and 
shape matching algorithms to identify decision boxes, processes, 
and terminators. Their research highlights the importance of 
handling noise and irregular strokes in hand-drawn diagrams to 
ensure robust structural extraction.~\cite{flowchart-analysis}

Furthermore, transformer-based models such as FR-DETR achieve high 
precision and recall by combining object detection with line 
segment detection in a unified architecture, reaching F1-scores 
above 90\% on benchmark datasets.
Despite these improvements, these systems remain computationally 
intensive and often struggle with real-world conditions such as 
hand-drawn diagrams, irregular shapes, and noisy backgrounds. 
Additionally, most OGR systems produce static outputs without 
interactive editing capabilities.~\cite{diagram-survey}

The proposed system addresses these limitations by leveraging 
multimodal AI models capable of contextual understanding, replacing 
rigid rule-based methods with flexible semantic interpretation.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:lit-conclusion}

%---------------------------------------------------------------

The literature review establishes the project's technical foundation and reveals gaps addressed by the Automated Diagram Digitization system. Utilizing Gemini AI, the system replaces heuristic methods with contextual multimodal understanding to identify shapes, text, and connections. It offers unique flexibility by supporting image uploads, natural language descriptions, and JSON imports in a single platform.

The platform also bridge the post-recognition editing gap through an 
interactive editor that supports drag-and-drop from a shape palette, 
snapshot-based undo/redo, hierarchical auto-layout, and edge labeling. 
It addresses common text formatting loss by providing bold, italic, 
and font size adjustments while preserving line breaks using 
\texttt{\textbackslash n} characters. Finally, the system extends 
beyond limited export options by offering PNG, SVG, and JSON formats, 
while enhancing user comfort and workflow continuity through 
theme switching and persistent PostgreSQL storage with UUID-based 
identification.

in isolation, the Automated Diagram Digitization platform integrates
